The 7-phase method

Every job runs through the same seven phases.

Built so nothing gets missed, and so you always know what's happening, hour by hour.

1 Arrival & Walkthrough 2 Setup & Staging 3 The Sort 4 Removal 5 Zone & Organize 6 Label & Document 7 Final Walkthrough
1

Arrival & Walkthrough

15–30 minutes

We show up on time, in the same truck you saw at the quote visit. Before we move anything, we tour the space with you and write the plan down. Most surprises in a cleanout happen in the first twenty minutes — we'd rather find them now than at hour four.

What we ask you

  • What absolutely stays, no matter what?
  • Anything with sentimental weight we should slow down for?
  • Anything fragile we should know about?
  • Where does the dust and noise bother you least?
  • How do you want to be involved — every decision, or just the hard ones?
PHASE 1 — handshake at the driveway, clipboard visible, garage door open
2

Setup & Staging

20–40 minutes

We lay down floor protection, set up staging areas in the driveway (or on a tarp inside, if the weather's bad), and pull anything blocking access. The garage gets worse before it gets better — but only for the next couple of hours.

PHASE 2 — driveway laid out with tarps, items being staged in piles
3

The Sort

2–3 hours · the main event

We work corner by corner, item by item. You make the calls; we hold the rules and the rhythm. Decision fatigue is the enemy of a good cleanout — our job is to keep you moving without ever making you feel rushed.

Two piles, that's it

  • Keep — goes back into the garage, zoned and labeled.
  • Dispose — everything you decide doesn't stay. We take it from there; you don't have to think about it.

Three rules we lean on

The 5-Year RuleIf you haven't touched it in five years, you don't own it — it owns you. Doesn't mean it goes, but it changes the default answer.
The Replacement RuleIf it broke tomorrow, would you spend money to replace it? If not, it's quietly already gone.
The Sentimentality FilterSentimental items deserve real consideration — not a yes-or-no question at hour three. We pull them aside and revisit them at the end, when you're calmer.
PHASE 3 — homeowner and one of us, hands on an item, two piles visible behind
4

Removal

1–2 hours · we step away, you breathe

Once the sort is done, the dispose pile becomes our problem. One of us takes the truck and handles everything in that pile from start to finish — you don't have to think about where it goes or what happens to it. The other one of us stays at the house, getting Phase 5 ready.

PHASE 4 — pickup truck loaded, tarp tied down, pulling out of the driveway
5

Zone & Organize

1–2 hours

The keepers come back in — but not where they were. We build the four-zone strategy that turns a garage from a dumping ground into a working tool.

Front-left · near the door

Active

Daily and weekly items live here. Eye-level, closest to the garage door so you grab and go.

  • Snow shovel & salt
  • Rakes, brooms, leaf bags
  • Recycling & green bins
  • Bike helmets & daily-use sports gear
PHASE 5 — labeled shelves being built, four zones marked on tape on the floor
6

Label & Document

30–45 minutes

Every shelf, every bin, every zone gets a label that's readable from a step back. We photograph the finished garage in a four-corner grid so you have a record of how it looked the day we left.

PHASE 6 — close-up of a labeled bin: "SEASONAL · holiday décor"
7

Final Walkthrough

15–20 minutes

We do the walkthrough together. We show you each zone, where things ended up, and why — so you can find anything you need on day one without guessing. You point at anything that's not where you want it, and we fix it on the spot. Then we settle up the agreed quote, and that's the day.

PHASE 7 — homeowner standing inside the finished garage, hands on hips, tired smile

How this started

One garage. One Saturday. One idea.

We've been friends since we were kids. The kind of friendship where you know each other's parents' phone numbers, and you've spent more summers in each other's basements than your own.

One weekend, David R's family decided his garage had to go. Years of stuff, no plan, a couple of dollar-store bins, and a lot of "we'll figure it out." We figured it out together — eight hours, two coffees, and a garage that suddenly made sense.

"Halfway through, we looked at each other and said: people would pay for this. And we could do it for them."

That's where this started. Not a business plan in a Word doc — a Saturday we couldn't stop talking about for a month.

We're not trying to build a chain. We're trying to build a name in our neighbourhood. A few hundred Thornhill garages over the next few years, done one at a time by the same two people, with our handshake and our reputation on every one of them.
